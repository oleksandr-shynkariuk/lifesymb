Proprioception is defined as:
Depth perception; kinaesthetic sensibility; perception of position and movement of our body.
Proprioception has three qualities:
1. Sense of position: The positions of our joints are exactly oriented. We can adjust the position of the elbow joint with great accuracy only by considering the position of the other arm (even with closed eyes).
2. Sense of movement: We perceive the movements of our limbs and joints by our proprioceptors. By doing so, we can control the body parts that are closer to our body better, than the more distant ones.
3. Sense of force: We must perceive the degree of strength that we apply in order to perform a movement or maintain a joint position.
When we get born we are only crawling on the ground and perceive our surroundings through the sensors in our skin. Muscles and connective tissue contain contractile fibres and receptors, which are tiny sensory organs. The ability of the body to sense changes in the position of the body is provided by so called proprioceptors. During physical injury, stress and threatening or stressful events the fibres pull together, which restricts the mobility. At the same time, the receptors report an uncomfortable feeling, pain, anxiety, nausea, or paraesthesia.
Conversely, the organism reacts on positive events with relaxation, mobility and well-being. Sensor and motor function, sensing and moving, regulate each other: the more discomfort the more tension and the more tension, the more paraesthesia.

Sensorimotor Disturbances
Depending on the location of the permanent contraction the temporary discomfort develops into massive complaints. These complaints can be:
- Headache and / or dizziness, but also "depressive thoughts" through tension in the neck and head muscles
- Knee pain during tension in the back, leg and foot muscles and / or the associated connective tissue. These tensions are formed as we raise us in a forced self-assertion.
- Bladder complaints occur during tensions in the lower abdomen and pelvic floor muscles (usually together with a fear tensioning in the front side of the body)
- Respiratory and / or cardiac disruptions associated with depression or anxiety
- Etc.
As we grow older our visual system dominates more and more over all other senses and we almost completely unlearn our prior abilities. The nervous system underlies the principle of “use it or lose it”. Whenever we stop using our senses, we will lose the ability to use them. On the other hand, when we continue using them, we can increase the quality of our life tremendously.

The Sensors Program
The Sensors program creates a stable stance and connection to the ground. A solid stance and a flawless posture is a fundamental ability for every task in life. The perception of a good position of the body in a 3-dimensional room provides safety and prevents injuries. Especially in the cold winter months there is nothing that can replace the control of the own movements against forces from outside.
